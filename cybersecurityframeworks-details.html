Cybersecurity Frameworks (Detailed)

NIST Cybersecurity Framework (NIST CSF)

The NIST CSF provides a **risk-based**, flexible, and scalable approach to managing cybersecurity threats. It is structured around five high-level functions:

  • Identify: Asset inventory, risk assessment, business environment.
  • Protect: Access control, encryption, awareness training, data security.
  • Detect: Continuous monitoring, anomaly detection, event analysis.
  • Respond: Incident management, mitigation, communication protocols.
  • Recover: Recovery planning, system restoration, resilience strengthening.

Widely used by government, critical infrastructure, and enterprises.

ISO/IEC 27001 (ISMS - International Standard)

ISO 27001 defines requirements for establishing an Information Security Management System (ISMS). It includes:

  • 114 controls across 14 domains (Annex A)
  • Mandatory risk assessment and treatment plan
  • Continuous improvement through PDCA (Plan-Do-Check-Act)
  • Certification recognized globally

It ensures confidentiality, integrity, and availability (CIA) of information.

CIS Critical Security Controls (v8)

The CIS Controls provide **18 prioritized safeguards** to defend against the most common cyber threats.

  • Asset Management
  • Secure Configuration
  • Vulnerability Management
  • Access Control
  • Incident Response

Designed for technical implementation and SOC teams.

MITRE ATT&CK Framework

MITRE ATT&CK is a detailed catalog of adversarial behaviors arranged into:

  • Tactics: High-level attacker goals (Privilege Escalation, Lateral Movement, etc.)
  • Techniques: Methods attackers use
  • Sub-Techniques: Step-by-step operations

Used for threat hunting, SOC operations, and red team exercises.

COBIT (Control Objectives for Information and Related Technologies)

COBIT focuses on **IT governance**, aligning cybersecurity and business objectives. It provides:

  • 40 governance and management objectives
  • Performance metrics and maturity models
  • Enterprise-wide risk and compliance management
Zero Trust Architecture (ZTA)

Zero Trust is based on “Never trust, always verify.” Key principles:

  • Continuous authentication & authorization
  • Device health verification
  • Least privilege access
  • Micro-segmentation

Regulatory Compliance Standards (Detailed)

GDPR (General Data Protection Regulation)

GDPR is the EU’s strongest privacy regulation. Key elements include:

  • Lawful basis for data processing
  • Rights to access, delete, and correct data
  • Data minimization & storage limitation
  • DPIAs (Data Protection Impact Assessments)
  • Strict fines for violations (up to 4% global revenue)
HIPAA (Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act)

HIPAA protects health information in the U.S. through:

  • Privacy Rule: Limits access to PHI
  • Security Rule: Safeguards for electronic PHI (ePHI)
  • Breach Notification Rule
  • Administrative, physical, technical safeguards
PCI DSS (Payment Card Industry Data Security Standard)

PCI DSS secures cardholder data through 12 major requirements, including:

  • Firewall configuration
  • Strong access control
  • Encryption of cardholder data
  • Anti-malware controls
  • Logging and monitoring
SOX (Sarbanes-Oxley Act)

SOX ensures financial transparency and protects investors from fraud. Cybersecurity is required to safeguard:

  • Financial reporting systems
  • Internal controls (Section 404)
  • Data integrity and audit trails
FedRAMP

A U.S. government standard for secure cloud services.

  • Baseline levels (Low, Moderate, High)
  • Continuous monitoring
  • Security Assessment Framework (SAF)
  • Authorization to Operate (ATO)
CCPA / CPRA (California Privacy Laws)

These laws give consumers strong rights over their personal data.

  • Right to opt-out of data selling
  • Right to access and delete information
  • Expanded sensitive data protections
  • Mandatory consumer disclosures
